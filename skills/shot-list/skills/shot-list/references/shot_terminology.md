# Shot Terminology Reference

## Shot Types (Distance from Subject)

### Extreme Wide Shot (EWS)
- **Also called**: Extreme Long Shot (ELS)
- **Shows**: Vast landscape/cityscape with subject very small
- **Use for**: Epic scope, isolation, establishing vast scale
- **Example**: Lone figure in desert, city skyline

### Wide Shot (WS)
- **Also called**: Long Shot (LS), Full Shot when showing full body
- **Shows**: Subject with significant environment
- **Use for**: Establishing location, geography, spatial relationships
- **Example**: Character entering a room, building exterior

### Medium Wide Shot (MWS)
- **Also called**: Medium Long Shot (MLS), Cowboy Shot (knees up)
- **Shows**: Subject from approximately knees up
- **Use for**: Action with body language, movement space
- **Example**: Western gunfights, walking conversations

### Medium Shot (MS)
- **Shows**: Subject from waist up
- **Use for**: Standard dialogue, balanced intimacy/context
- **Example**: Interview framing, casual conversation

### Medium Close-Up (MCU)
- **Shows**: Subject from mid-chest up
- **Use for**: Dialogue with emphasis, light emotional moments
- **Example**: News anchor framing, focused conversation

### Close-Up (CU)
- **Shows**: Face filling frame, or object detail
- **Use for**: Emotion, reaction, important detail
- **Example**: Emotional revelation, reading a letter

### Extreme Close-Up (ECU)
- **Also called**: XCU, Macro
- **Shows**: Portion of face (eyes, lips) or tiny detail
- **Use for**: Intense emotion, critical detail, tension
- **Example**: Eye contact, finger on trigger, tear dropping

### Insert (INS)
- **Shows**: Object or detail cut into scene
- **Use for**: Props, text, hands, important objects
- **Example**: Clock face, text message, hand grabbing key

## Camera Angles

### Eye Level
- **Camera position**: Same height as subject's eyes
- **Effect**: Neutral, natural, relatable
- **Use for**: Standard coverage, audience identification

### Low Angle
- **Camera position**: Below subject, looking up
- **Effect**: Power, dominance, heroic, intimidating
- **Use for**: Villains, heroes in triumph, tall buildings

### High Angle
- **Camera position**: Above subject, looking down
- **Effect**: Vulnerability, smallness, overview
- **Use for**: Victims, defeat, establishing geography

### Dutch Angle (Canted/Tilted)
- **Camera position**: Rotated on axis, horizon tilted
- **Effect**: Unease, disorientation, psychological instability
- **Use for**: Horror, thriller tension, altered states

### Bird's Eye View
- **Camera position**: Directly overhead
- **Effect**: God-like perspective, pattern emphasis
- **Use for**: Dance sequences, crime scenes, symmetry

### Worm's Eye View
- **Camera position**: Ground level looking up
- **Effect**: Extreme power, looming presence
- **Use for**: Approaching danger, awe-inspiring subjects

## Camera Movement

### Static
- **Movement**: None (tripod/locked)
- **Effect**: Stability, observation, formal
- **Use for**: Dialogue coverage, interviews, precision framing

### Pan
- **Movement**: Horizontal rotation on fixed axis
- **Effect**: Reveal, follow action, survey space
- **Use for**: Following subject, revealing environment

### Tilt
- **Movement**: Vertical rotation on fixed axis
- **Effect**: Reveal height, survey vertically
- **Use for**: Tall buildings, character head-to-toe

### Dolly
- **Movement**: Camera moves toward/away from subject
- **Dolly In**: Increasing intimacy, emphasis, tension
- **Dolly Out**: Revealing context, pulling away, ending
- **Use for**: Emotional emphasis, dramatic reveals

### Tracking/Traveling
- **Movement**: Camera moves parallel to subject
- **Effect**: Following action, maintaining composition
- **Use for**: Walking conversations, chase scenes

### Crane/Jib
- **Movement**: Camera moves up/down and potentially horizontally
- **Effect**: Epic scale, dramatic reveal, establishing
- **Use for**: Opening/closing shots, reveals, scope

### Handheld
- **Movement**: Operator-controlled, visible shake
- **Effect**: Documentary realism, tension, energy, chaos
- **Use for**: Action, horror, intense drama, found footage

### Steadicam/Gimbal
- **Movement**: Smooth operator movement, no shake
- **Effect**: Fluid following, dreamy, controlled energy
- **Use for**: Long takes, following characters, elegance

### Zoom
- **Movement**: Lens focal length change (no physical movement)
- **Effect**: Dramatic emphasis, unnatural feel, tension
- **Use for**: Emphasis (often combined with dolly for "Vertigo effect")

### Push In
- **Movement**: Moving toward subject (dolly or zoom)
- **Effect**: Focusing attention, building intensity
- **Use for**: Dramatic moments, realizations

### Pull Out
- **Movement**: Moving away from subject
- **Effect**: Revealing context, distancing, endings
- **Use for**: Reveals, conclusions, isolation

## Composition Types

### Two-Shot (2S)
- Two subjects in frame
- Use for: Conversations, relationships, confrontation

### Three-Shot (3S)
- Three subjects in frame
- Use for: Group dynamics, triangular relationships

### Over-the-Shoulder (OTS)
- Camera behind one subject, showing other's face
- Use for: Dialogue coverage, POV suggestion

### Point of View (POV)
- Camera shows what character sees
- Use for: Subjective experience, tension, mystery

### Reaction Shot
- Subject responding to off-screen event/dialogue
- Use for: Emotional beats, comedy timing

### Cutaway
- Shot of something other than main action
- Use for: Transitions, time passage, B-roll

### Clean Single
- One subject without other characters in frame
- Use for: Focus, isolation, separate coverage

### Dirty Single
- One subject with part of another in frame
- Use for: Maintaining spatial relationship

## Shot Modifiers/Extensions

| Notation | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| (V.O.) | Voice Over - character heard but not seen |
| (O.S.) | Off Screen - character in scene but not in shot |
| (O.C.) | Off Camera - same as O.S. |
| FAVORING | Composition emphasizes one subject |
| FEATURING | Shot includes specific element |
| ANGLE ON | Shift focus to specific subject |
| ANOTHER ANGLE | Same scene, different composition |
| REVERSE | Opposite direction of previous shot |
| MATCHING | Mirrors composition of previous shot |
